I get this question at least twice a week: *Gavin, should I do real grass or artificial turf?*

And my honest answer is — it depends. But more often than not, I steer Orange County homeowners toward artificial turf. Here's why.

## The Water Reality

Orange County averages about 13 inches of rain per year. That's desert-adjacent. Real sod needs supplemental irrigation year-round, and with tiered water rates in most SoCal municipalities, a 500 sq ft lawn can add $80–$120 to your water bill every month during summer.

Artificial turf needs zero irrigation once it's in. The math works itself out pretty fast.

## What Most People Get Wrong About Turf

The biggest objection I hear is *"it looks fake."* And ten years ago, that was fair. Today's turf products — especially the nylon/polyethylene blends at 50–60 oz face weight — are genuinely hard to distinguish from real grass at normal viewing distance. I've had homeowners forget we installed it six months later.

The key is in the infill. Cheap installations skip proper infill or use crumb rubber, which gets hot and breaks down. We use a sand and organic infill blend that keeps surface temps manageable and gives the turf the right give underfoot.

## Where Sod Still Wins

I'll always recommend sod when:
- The client has kids or dogs who are hard on turf and need the durability of natural grass root systems
- The yard has significant shade (turf drains heat poorly in shaded areas with poor airflow)
- Budget is very tight — sod is $6.98/sq ft installed vs. $7.98/sq ft for turf, but turf pays back in 2–3 years on water savings alone

## What the Install Actually Looks Like

A proper turf install isn't just rolling out a carpet. We excavate 3–4 inches, install a decomposed granite or crushed aggregate base, compact it, lay weed barrier, then cut and seam the turf before anchoring the perimeter and applying infill. Done right, it should last 15–20 years with minimal maintenance.

If you're getting bids and someone quotes you a same-day install with no excavation — walk away.
