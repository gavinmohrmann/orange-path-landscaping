There's a certain kind of front yard that stops you mid-step. Not because it's showy or sculpted. But because it looks like the land just *decided* to do that there.

That's what I'm always chasing.

In the coastal hills of Orange County — with that marine layer rolling in most mornings, the decomposed granite hillsides, the amber light by late afternoon — there's no reason to fight the landscape. The land already knows what it wants to grow. Our job is to listen, then help it say it a little louder.

---

## Start with What's Already There

Before a single plant goes in the ground, I walk the site. I look at the grade, where the drainage wants to go, what's growing in the parkway strips and cracks of the curb. In coastal OC that means coastal sage scrub — black sage, white sage, lemonade berry, toyon, deer grass. These aren't exotic choices. These are the plants that evolved here and want to grow here.

Echo those cues in a designed front yard and something almost magic happens: it stops looking installed and starts looking inevitable.

---

## The Plant Palette That Works

**Anchors:**
- *Salvia leucophylla* (Purple Sage) — silvery, aromatic, billowy if you let it get big
- *Rhus integrifolia* (Lemonade Berry) — dark glossy leaves, rugged, one of the most underused large shrubs in SoCal
- *Toyon* (Heteromeles arbutifolia) — red berries in winter, the plant that gave Hollywood its name

**Mid-layer:**
- *Muhlenbergia rigens* (Deer Grass) — moves in the wind, softens the space between boulders and shrubs better than anything
- *Encelia californica* (Coast Sunflower) — fast, cheerful, self-seeds gently
- *Salvia apiana* (White Sage) — sculptural, fragrant, pollinators love it

**Ground level:**
- Decomposed granite in a warm gold tone — stable but not concrete-hard
- *Achillea millefolium* (Yarrow) drifting between rocks
- Creeping *Ceanothus* tucked against boulders, blurring the edge between plant and stone

---

## Boulders Are Architecture, Not Decoration

When people say they want a natural look, they often mean round river rocks scattered in mulch. That's not natural — that's a craft store.

What reads as natural is *mass*. Stone that looks like it came from somewhere and decided to stay. I work with large boulders — 200 to 400 pounds or more — set with one-third buried, tilted slightly, planted tight against so the roots seem to wrap the rock over time. When you get it right, you can't tell if it was placed two months ago or two hundred years ago.

---

## Pathways That Don't Announce Themselves

Concrete says *house*. Flagstone says *garden*. I almost always use irregular Pennsylvania or Arizona flagstone — warm earthy tones, natural edges — set in decomposed granite so plants can creep into the joints over time.

The spacing matters. Close enough to walk comfortably, but with a rhythm that slows you down slightly, makes you notice the sage brushing your ankle as you pass. That's not an accident. I design for it.

---

## The Hardest Part: Restraint

Most native front yards don't look right because people plant too much, too evenly, too soon. Native plants take two to three years to settle into what they're actually going to be. That first summer they look sparse. Clients get nervous. I always say: give it time.

The gaps are part of it. The negative space between clumps, the bare DG, the exposed boulder — that's breathing room. That's what makes the whole thing feel like it belongs to the land.

---

## Why It Matters

The water table in Southern California cannot support the landscapes most people grew up thinking are normal. Native landscapes use a fraction of the water, support local pollinators and birds, build soil health, and need no fertilizer.

I'm not a purist — a Meyer lemon near the front door is fine. But the bones should be native. Because when you get it right, when the salvia is silver in the morning fog and the deer grass is bending in the offshore wind and the toyon berries are coming in red against the November sky — you're not just looking at a pretty yard.

You're looking at California doing what California does. And there is nothing more beautiful than that.
