## The Starting Point

The homeowners — a couple who'd been in their Dana Point home for about six years — called me in early October. Their front lawn was done. Not struggling, not patchy. Done. The grass had been slowly dying for two years, they'd cut back watering after the rate hikes, and honestly, it just wasn't worth saving anymore.

The yard was small — about 850 square feet total, which is pretty typical for that neighborhood. Mostly flat with a slight grade toward the street. They had an old pop-up sprinkler system that was probably installed in the late 90s, a few overgrown boxwood hedges along the foundation, and one decent-sized queen palm off to the left that we agreed to keep.

Their ask: something modern, low-water, and low-maintenance. They'd seen a few desert-modern yards around the area and liked the clean lines, the decomposed granite, the bold plants. They didn't want it to look like a cactus garden from a highway median. Fair enough.

Budget was $22,000 all-in. Realistic for what they wanted.

---

## Week One: Demo and Rough Grading

We started on a Monday. Four guys, two days of demo.

First thing we pulled out was the old sod — or what was left of it. We use a sod cutter for this when the grass has any life left, but in this case it was so dried out we just raked and shoveled it into the dump trailer. Probably 4 yards of debris hauled off.

The boxwoods came out next. They were overgrown, root-bound, and honestly dated the whole look. We cut them at the base, ground the stumps down, and dug out as much of the root mass as we could by hand. No herbicide — the homeowners didn't want it, and I rarely push for it on jobs this size.

Then we pulled the old irrigation system. Every head, every lateral line, the old valve box — all of it. The main supply line from the meter was still good, so we capped that and left it for the new system.

By Wednesday we were doing rough grading. We added about 2 yards of decomposed native soil to raise a planting berm on the right side of the yard — nothing dramatic, maybe 10 inches at the peak. It breaks up the flatness and gives the plants something to work with visually.

By Friday, the yard was a clean slate. Bare dirt, good grade, ready to build.

## The Design: What We Chose and Why

Here's where I'll get specific, because this is where a lot of desert-modern projects go wrong. People go too heavy on rock and too light on plants, and the yard ends up looking like a parking lot with a cactus in it.

The plan we landed on:

- **Decomposed granite** (3" depth, stabilized) across roughly 500 sq ft of the main open area — we used a warm gray/tan blend, not the orange-red DG that reads as cheap
- **Concrete stepping path** down the center, 18" x 24" slabs set in the DG with 1.5" gaps for a clean, intentional look
- **A planting berm** on the right side with a mix of agave, deer grass, and one 15-gallon Palo Verde tree as the anchor
- **Ground cover** along the left foundation: low-growing trailing rosemary and a few clumps of blue oat grass
- **Existing queen palm** stayed — we just cleaned it up, removed the dead fronds, and gave it a good watering stake

The plant list wasn't exotic or expensive. Here's roughly what we planted and what we paid at wholesale:

- 1x 15-gallon Palo Verde — $85
- 3x 5-gallon Blue Agave — $28 each
- 2x 5-gallon Foxtail Agave — $32 each
- 6x 1-gallon Deer Grass — $12 each
- 8x 1-gallon Trailing Rosemary — $10 each
- 4x 1-gallon Blue Oat Grass — $11 each

Total plant material: around $450 wholesale. Retail would be maybe $750-900 at a nursery. That's it. You don't need to spend a fortune on plants to get a strong look — you need good placement and the right backdrop.

## Week Two: Hardscape and Irrigation

Week two was the heavy lifting.

We poured the stepping stone path on Tuesday — formed it ourselves, poured a 3.5" slab, scored the joints. Simple, clean, no sealer. In my opinion, an unsealed concrete path weathers better and looks more intentional in a desert landscape than the glossy stuff.

We also installed two new drip zones — one for the berm plantings, one for the foundation bed. We used half-inch poly mainline with individual emitters at each plant, plus a pressure regulator at the valve. The whole system ties into their existing controller, which had an open zone. That saved them probably $400 versus running a new controller.

The DG went down on Thursday and Friday. This is a two-step process: compacted base layer first, then the finish layer on top. We use a plate compactor on the base — don't skip this step or you'll get soft spots and ruts within a year. Total DG used: about 7.5 tons delivered.

One thing I always do on DG installs: we lay a good commercial-grade weed barrier underneath. Not the cheap stuff from a big box store. The cheap stuff degrades in about two years and you end up with a mess. We use a 4-oz non-woven polypropylene fabric. It costs more but it works.

---

## Week Three: Planting, Finish Work, and Cleanup

Planting went fast — one full day for two guys.

We set the Palo Verde first to establish the height anchor, then worked outward. Agaves went in next — we spaced them wider than felt natural at first. That's intentional. Desert plants need room to grow, and if you crowd them at install, you're either looking at a cramped yard in three years or a re-do. The deer grass and rosemary filled in the lower layers.

We top-dressed the berm with a 2" layer of crushed granite mulch — a slightly darker tone than the DG to help the planting areas read separately from the hardscape zones.

Final irrigation check, adjusted all emitter flow rates, ran each zone twice to confirm coverage.

On the last day, we did a final grade cleanup, edged the DG borders with 4" steel edging, and planted a small agave near the base of the palm as a grounding element.

The homeowners walked the yard with me on day 21. Their reaction was exactly what you want — quiet for a second, then "this is so much better than I thought it was going to be."

## What This Project Cost, Broken Down

I'll be straight with you:

- Demo and hauling: ~$1,800
- Grading and soil: ~$900
- Concrete path: ~$2,200
- DG material and install: ~$3,400
- Weed barrier: ~$380
- Steel edging: ~$320
- Irrigation (new drip system, two zones): ~$1,600
- Plant material and planting labor: ~$2,800
- General labor across three weeks: ~$7,200
- Cleanup, final grade, misc: ~$400

**Total: $21,000**

Came in $1,000 under budget. That doesn't always happen, but demo went smooth and we didn't hit any irrigation surprises under the slab.

## What I'd Tell Any Dana Point or South OC Homeowner Thinking About This

A few honest takeaways:

- **Don't save a dead lawn.** Sod replacement in this climate is expensive and temporary. If you're already here, make the switch.
